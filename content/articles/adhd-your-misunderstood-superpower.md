Hey! You! Yeah you — do you feel like a square peg trying to fit into a round hole your whole life?

Are you used to being called stupid, irresponsible, lazy, or spacey… a dreamer who can't get anything done? Even if it's just by the voices in your head?

Do other people say you're "too much" — you feel too much, talk too much, do too much… are generally just *too much person* to deal with?

Can you relate with having 100 thoughts or ideas all screaming for attention at once, yet you can't focus long enough to follow through with any of them?

If you connect with any of this, you may have Attention Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD).

But don't let the word "disorder" fool you. Trail blazers with ADHD such as John Lennon, Michael Jordan, and Leonardo da Vinci have made history being their different, unique, and stupendous selves. By learning more about this condition and finding ways to optimize it, ADHD can become your **SUPERPOWER.**

## Your Different and Wonderful ADHD Brain

The brain of an ADHD person is structurally and functionally different from that of a person without the condition. Structurally, the prefrontal cortex, cerebellum, hippocampus, and amygdala tend to be smaller in people with ADHD. Functionally, this can cause poor memory, emotional instability, distractibility, and difficulty with complex problem solving.

People with ADHD have abnormally low levels of the neurotransmitter dopamine transmitting between the prefrontal cortex and basal ganglia. Since a neurotransmitter is essentially a messenger that communicates to different parts of the brain, not having enough messengers means the right signals don't get to the right places at the right time. This results in the symptoms characteristic of ADHD.

## How Your Brain Got This Way

Evidence points to ADHD having a genetic component (76% heritable) which can be turned on or intensified by certain environmental factors — often coming into play early in the womb if mom used alcohol, smoked, was excessively stressed, or was exposed to environmental toxins like lead.

While roughly 9% of kids diagnosed with ADHD grow out of it, the majority of kids become adults with the condition.

## ADHD Types Explained

### Inattentive Type

- Trouble staying focused and sustaining mental effort
- Spacey-ness or perpetual daydreaming
- Easily distracted; has trouble finishing tasks
- Forgets routine chores (like paying bills)
- Has trouble following instructions

### Hyperactive/Impulsive Type

- Constant movement — can't sit still, especially in quiet settings
- Excessive fidgeting
- Can't stop talking; impulsively interrupts or blurts out answers
- Can't wait their turn
- Struggles with self-control; acts without thinking

Individuals can be diagnosed with ADHD presenting as Inattentive, Hyperactive, or **Both.**

## The Risks of Not Seeking Treatment

Half of individuals with undiagnosed ADHD will develop a substance use disorder at some point in their lives. The reason: those with ADHD have brains that produce less dopamine, so they crave it and find ways to get it one way or another.

Those with ADHD are 4 times more likely to experience depression in their lives. When left untreated, the individual experiences frustration, disappointment, and low self-esteem because they cannot function in the same way as others.

## Treatment Synergy

Often pharmaceuticals are the go-to treatment for ADHD. The ADHD brain has a lack of dopamine; stimulants increase dopamine levels, thereby improving brain function. This doesn't come without side effects, however, which can include loss of appetite, difficulty sleeping, anxiety, and changes in blood pressure and heart rate.

Even those put on medication still need other therapies to create a comprehensive plan. There is no quick fix pill.

## Turn Your Symptoms into Superpowers

Stimulants are not the only way to improve dopamine levels. Intense exercise done regularly increases baseline dopamine as well — which may explain why 8-10% of athletes have ADHD compared to the general population (around 3-5%).

**Other effective tools without medication:**

**Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — Helps people with ADHD become aware of self-critical thoughts that have been on replay for years, creating changes to thought processes and in your relationship to yourself.

**Mindfulness Training** — 5-10 minutes of mindfulness can go a long way in managing symptoms. Research shows this practice improves attention while lessening distractibility.

**Create Structure** — A calendar and a to-do list are two essential tools for executive functioning. They help you manage your time, remember appointments, and stick to important tasks.

**Find an Activity Partner** — You don't have to do it alone. Find a friend, family member, or co-worker who could also use some accountability. Check in with each other throughout the week.

**Get Your Sleep Schedule Dialed** — Those with ADHD are more likely to experience sleep problems. Lack of sleep intensifies symptoms. Develop a night-time ritual that decreases stimulation (screens) and includes meditation or yoga.

**Focus on Nutrition** — Decrease sugar, caffeine, and carbohydrates while always having protein-rich snacks on hand like nuts, cheese, and peanut butter. Feed yourself a balanced diet, increase water intake, and your brain will function much more optimally.
