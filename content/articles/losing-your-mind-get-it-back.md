If you're American, you probably feel like you are losing your mind from time to time — or maybe even all the time. It's no wonder. Our modern age is full of constant stimulation, mega stressors, and much more happening at a breakneck pace. 50 million Americans struggle with mental illness. 1 in 5 American adults will have a diagnosable mental health condition every year, with anxiety disorders being the most common.

If this sounds like you, there are 5 simple things you can implement today that will improve your mental and physical health. Make these practices habits, and you will notice your quality of life improve *tremendously* and immediately.

## #1 Create Space in Your Brain

Overwhelm happens when there is too much going on in our brains and not enough space to process it all. Remember how on the old computers there was an hourglass that came up when the computer was thinking? It's the same with our brain — we can only hold so much information for so long before the system starts to lag. And that lag can manifest as anxiety, depression, and poor concentration.

**Meditation** is one of those things that everyone knows the benefits of but many won't do. Most people say "I can't sit still for that long" or "Doing nothing seems pointless." But you can't know how awesome meditation is until you've experienced it.

There is an overwhelming amount of evidence supporting improvements to anxiety disorders, depression, addiction, and stress management through meditation practices. People who meditate regularly report significant increases in focus, the ability to manage stress, and the ability to calm anxious thoughts — all from "just sitting there."

**How to start:** Sit in a chair or on the floor in a comfortable but pleasantly alert position. Become aware of your breath and the sensations of your body. As thoughts return to your brain, become aware of them — but don't chase them or problem-solve. Let the thoughts come and go like clouds in the sky as you stay centered in the present moment, feeling your breath.

If you do that for 10 minutes a day, even just 4 days a week, your life will change.

## #2 Green Spaces: Phytoncides and Forest Bathing

Spend time in nature to improve your mental health. It's not just a cute sentiment — it's a scientific fact. An increasing amount of research is being done on the health benefits of literally just standing around in a forest, or "forest bathing" as the Japanese call it.

Breathing in the wood essential oils from trees, called phytoncides, not only boosts the immune system and lowers blood pressure — it has a profound impact on mental health. Researchers found that forest bathing significantly decreased anxiety, depression, anger, confusion, and fatigue in participants.

**Grounding** is another profound but often overlooked technique — making contact with the earth without anything in between, such as shoes or concrete. The earth has an abundance of free electrons which our bodies use to neutralize free radicals when we ground. These free electrons reduce pain and inflammation, improve sleep, decrease depression and anxiety, and improve overall health.

So take those shoes off and walk in the grass for a while. Science supports you.

## #3 Do Something "Meaningless" (AKA Have Fun!)

Serotonin is released when chilling out and having fun. Serotonin is also the antidote to cortisol — the stress chemical that ramps us up and eventually causes inflammation and tissue breakdown.

When we have enough serotonin, we experience relaxation, pleasure, joy, peace, positivity, and a willingness to connect with others. It helps stave off anxiety and depression, and even helps to heal wounds. Which means in a way, screwing off can literally help you heal faster.

What is fun to you? If you can't think of anything, go back to when you were young. What made you happy? Take note of all the things that are fun for you and pursue them passionately. Your health depends on it.

## #4 The Power of People

Whether you like it or not, the benefits we receive from connecting with friends, family, and other people in general cannot be underestimated. Just ask 2020. We are social creatures by design. Humans isolating for safety is like a goldfish climbing a tree for air.

An article published in the *American Journal of Lifestyle Medicine* states that "there is significant evidence that social support and feeling connected can decrease depressive symptoms, mitigate PTSD symptoms, and improve overall mental health."

A meta-analysis from Brigham Young University reviewed 148 articles on the effects of human interactions on health outcomes. They found that interactions with friends, family, and colleagues increase a person's chance of survival by a whopping **50%**. Social isolation was found to be more harmful to one's health than smoking cigarettes, drinking alcohol, not exercising, and being obese.

If you're feeling isolated, it's time to put yourself out there. Get involved in community events, start volunteering, pick up a new hobby, take classes at the local college, start saying hi to your neighbors.

## #5 Train Your Brain

Do you sit on the couch eating bon-bons and watching TV all night, then wake up the next morning expecting to see a six-pack in the mirror? Of course not. So why do you expect to have stable mental health when you never prioritize it?

You've just been given 5 easy ways to make your experience of life a lot better. Don't try to do them all at once — pick one. It doesn't matter which. Do it for a month and see how it goes. You are the only person with the power to change your life.

Now you have the tools. Go change yourself to change the world.
