You've probably heard of Multiple Personality Disorder? Well, according to western medicine that no longer exists. Now it's referred to as DID. Thought of throughout the ages as demon possession, Dissociative Identity Disorder is one of the most stigmatized and misunderstood mental health conditions there is.

Now widely understood as a result of extreme childhood trauma, it occurs in about 1% of the population. The individual displays multiple, dissociative personalities that come out at various times, making them appear to have different people living inside of them.

Although Hollywood portrays this condition as scary and violent, DID people are actually less violent than the general population, often turning their anger in on themselves, causing suicidal thoughts, behaviors, and self-harming practices.

## What is Dissociative Identity Disorder?

Dissociative Identity Disorder is characterized by the presence of two or more distinct identities or personality states within an individual. These identities, often referred to as alters, differ in behaviors, attitudes, memories, and even physical characteristics. The transitions between these identities are often accompanied by memory gaps and a sense of detachment from reality.

DID can have a wide range of symptoms that manifest differently from case to case. Folks with it experience amnesia, identity fragmentation, oddly distinct shifts in behavior, voice, or mannerisms, and some experience hearing internal voices or conversations. Individuals with DID often struggle with multiple conditions simultaneously — such as depression, anxiety disorders, self-harm, and substance abuse — which can complicate establishing DID as a diagnosis.

## What is Dissociation?

Like most things in health, dissociation exists as a spectrum. On the low end, we've all experienced some form of dissociation. **Dissociation is a mental process where a person disconnects from their thoughts, feelings, memories, or sense of identity.**

I bet you have done things automatically that you've done hundreds of times, like pumping gas or driving to work. While on autopilot, we often space out and think of other things. This is a mild form of dissociation.

But this experience goes to a whole different level in people with DID. A hallmark of someone living with DID is having chunks of time that are just unaccounted for. Blank places in their memory. Often DID people will blank out completely when a different personality takes over, and not be able to recall what happened while this other personality was present. It's almost like each personality has its own separate memory bank.

Dissociation can also be associated with out-of-body experiences — feeling detached from their own body, their emotions, or even from the environment they're in.

## Why Dissociation Happens

People who develop DID experience repetitive and often extreme trauma, be it physical and/or emotional, at a very early age — often before age 10. Since as children DID people were unable to get away from the situation physically, their mind created other states of consciousness to allow them to dissociate from the pain and "be" somewhere else — and eventually, someone else.

Each of these personalities or alters were at one point in time created to protect the child who could not protect themselves.

**Common manifestations of alters/protectors:**

- **Host** — The part that has control of the body most of the time and has no/few traumatic memories.
- **Child** — The little version of you (often called "littles"), stuck at the age of the abuse.
- **Protector** — Deals with the situation in times of perceived danger; can harbor fear, anger, and sadness.
- **Caretaker** — Cares for other alters, nurturing the younger and more vulnerable ones.
- **Gatekeeper** — Often appearing emotionless; decides which alter comes out and which memories are accessed.
- **Persecutors** — Anger and self-hate cause this alter to harm the others.
- **Non-human** — Fairies, animals, ghosts, created as a way to feel strong and capable as something else.
- **Fragments** — An undeveloped alter that holds only one memory or emotion.

## All Alters Are Protective

Do you see how each alter type serves to protect the child in some way? The protector is self-explanatory. The child lives in denial so as to live the life they should have had. The caretaker is a stand-in for the mothering one should have had. The gatekeeper chooses the best coping strategy for the situation. The sexual alter keeps sexual trauma locked away into one compartment. A fragment does its protecting by locking the worst memories into a nearly contextless fragment of the self so they hurt less.

The importance of the protective aspects of these alters cannot be overstated and is foundational to integrating alters.

## Treatment: Work Together as a Team

Treatment for individuals with DID generally involves long-term therapy aimed at promoting integration and cohesiveness among different identities. Psychotherapy — specifically trauma-focused approaches such as CBT and EMDR — are commonly used.

**Trauma-focused therapy** addresses the underlying traumatic experiences that led to the development of DID. **Integration therapy** promotes communication, cooperation, and understanding between identities. **Grounding techniques** — meditation, mindfulness, grounding exercises — help individuals manage dissociative symptoms by regulating the nervous system.

There is no medication for dissociative disorders. Some clinicians will treat the accompanying depression and anxiety with medication to ensure safety and stability, but medication alone will never solve the problem.

**Creative therapies** — art therapy, music therapy, dance therapy — can also be beneficial, providing alternative channels for individuals to explore and communicate their thoughts, emotions, and experiences.

## No Shame

Experiencing and remembering trauma often causes the victim to feel guilt. It's almost as if they feel they deserved what happened to them. The truth is, the traumatic events you experienced were **NEVER** your fault. They were the result of a mentally ill person who took their issues out on the most vulnerable person in the situation — you.

So if you're a DID person, remember: it was NOT your fault. Seek help from friends and family, and seek treatment. Find a trusted, DID-experienced therapist that you feel genuinely seen with.
